Refrigerator

  • Unpack the refrigerator: Carefully remove any packaging materials, including tape, plastic, or foam, from the refrigerator. Be cautious while handling sharp objects to avoid damage.
  • Install shelves and accessories: Check the refrigerator's user manual for instructions on how to install shelves, drawers, and other accessories that come with the appliance. Follow the manufacturer's guidelines for proper placement and adjustment.
  • Level the refrigerator: Most refrigerators have adjustable feet or rollers at the bottom. Use a level tool to ensure that the fridge is level from front to back and side to side. This is important for proper functioning and to prevent doors from swinging open or not sealing properly.
  • Plug it in: Locate the power cord at the back of the refrigerator and plug it into a grounded electrical outlet. Avoid using extension cords if possible, as they may not provide sufficient power.
  • Allow for proper ventilation: Make sure there is enough space around the refrigerator for air circulation. Maintain a gap of several inches between the back and sides of the fridge and any walls or cabinets.
  • Set the temperature: Consult the refrigerator's user manual to adjust the temperature settings. Typically, there are control knobs or buttons located inside the refrigerator or on the exterior near the top or back.
  • Allow it to stabilize: Once powered on, it may take a few hours for the refrigerator to reach the desired temperature. Avoid overloading it with food initially, and give it time to stabilize before loading it up completely.
  • Check the door seals: Ensure that the refrigerator doors close properly and that the seals are intact. You can test the seal by closing the door on a sheet of paper and trying to pull it out. If the paper slips out easily, the seal may need adjustment or replacement.

Microwave

  • Choose the appropriate dishware: Ensure that the container or plate you're using is microwave-safe. Avoid using any dishes with metallic or silver accents as they can cause sparks and damage the microwave.
  • Prepare the food: Place the food you want to heat or cook inside the microwave-safe container. You can cover it with a microwave-safe lid or microwave-safe plastic wrap if needed.
  • Set the power level: If you want to adjust the power level, check your microwave's control panel for a power level button. You can typically select options like high, medium, or low power. If you don't adjust the power level, the microwave will default to the highest setting.
  • Switch on the power button.
  • Set the cooking time: Enter the desired cooking time using the microwave's number pad. Most microwaves have buttons for adding seconds or minutes. Press start or the appropriate button to initiate the cooking process.
  • Press the START button built initially on the door of the microwave.( start ,stop,some default time sets)
  • Monitor the cooking process: Keep an eye on the food while it's cooking. If you notice any unusual smells, sounds, or smoke, stop the microwave immediately. These could indicate an issue with the microwave or the container you're using.
  • Pause and stir (if necessary): If you're heating food for an extended period or cooking something that requires stirring, the microwave may prompt you to pause and stir the food halfway through the cooking time. Follow the instructions accordingly.
  • Let it stand: After the cooking time is complete, some microwaves may beep to indicate that the process is finished. Leave the food in the microwave for a short time (usually a minute or two) to allow for additional cooking and heat distribution.
  • Remove the food: Open the microwave door carefully, as the container and food may be hot. Use oven mitts or other protective gear to handle hot dishes. Take the food out of the microwave and place it on a heat-resistant surface.

Electric Geyser

  • Determine the Location: Choose a suitable location for your geyser, preferably near the area where you require hot water. It should have proper ventilation and easy access for maintenance.
  • Safety Measures: Before installing the geyser, ensure that you have the necessary safety features in place. This typically includes a circuit breaker or fuse to protect against electrical overloads, as well as a thermostat to control the temperature of the water.
  • Mounting: Mount the geyser on a sturdy wall using appropriate brackets or hangers. Make sure it is securely fixed to prevent any accidents.
  • Plumbing Connections: Connect the water supply pipes to the geyser. Typically, you'll have a cold water inlet and a hot water outlet. Ensure the connections are tight to avoid leaks.
  • Electrical Connection: Follow the manufacturer's instructions and hire a licensed electrician if needed. The geyser will require an electrical connection to a power source. Ensure that the wiring and electrical components are compatible with the geyser's power requirements.
  • Grounding: It's essential to ground the geyser for safety purposes. The grounding wire should be properly connected to the geyser and the grounding system of your home.
  • Thermostat Settings: Set the desired temperature on the geyser's thermostat. Make sure it is not set too high to prevent scalding accidents.
  • Water Tank Filling: Open the water supply valve to fill the geyser tank with cold water. Allow it to fill completely before turning on the geyser.
  • Power On: Switch on the geyser's power supply using the provided controls. The heating element will start heating the water.
  • Wait for Heating: Depending on the capacity of the geyser and the initial water temperature, it may take some time for the water to reach the desired temperature. Be patient while the geyser heats the water.
  • Hot Water Usage: Once the water has reached the desired temperature, you can use the hot water outlet to access the heated water.
